Complete Guide: Learn SCRUM in 8 Steps

Paso1: Fundamentals of agile technology

Agile technology is a way of working, mainly used in software development, that focuses on flexibility and continuous delivery instead of following a rigid plan from the beginning. It is based on the Agile Manifesto, which emphasizes valuing people, collaboration with customers, working results, and the ability to respond to change. In Agile, work is done in short cycles called iterations, where each cycle produces a small but functional part of the product, allowing teams to receive constant feedback and improve continuously. Customers are involved throughout the entire process, not just at the start or end. Agile also promotes teamwork through small, collaborative, and self-organizing groups that aim to improve their performance over time. Another key aspect is adaptability, as changes are welcomed and used to enhance the product rather than being avoided. Different frameworks such as Scrum, Kanban, and Extreme Programming (XP) are commonly used to apply Agile principles, but all of them share the same goal of delivering value in a flexible and collaborative way.

Paso2: What is SCRUM?

Scrum is a lightweight Agile framework used to manage and complete complex projects, especially in software development. It is based on the principles of the Agile Manifesto and focuses on teamwork, adaptability, and delivering value in small, continuous increments. Instead of trying to build a complete product all at once, Scrum divides the work into short cycles called “Sprints,” which usually last up to one month. At the end of each Sprint, the team delivers a usable part of the product. In Scrum, there is a small, self-organizing team made up of three main roles: the Product Owner, who defines what needs to be built and prioritizes the work; the Scrum Master, who helps the team follow Scrum and removes obstacles; and the Developers, who create the product. The team works together closely and is responsible for achieving a shared goal. Scrum also includes key events that help structure the work and ensure continuous improvement. These include Sprint Planning (to decide what to do), the Daily Scrum (a short daily meeting to track progress), the Sprint Review (to show the work to stakeholders), and the Sprint Retrospective (to improve how the team works). Overall, Scrum helps teams stay organized, adapt quickly to changes, and deliver high-quality results step by step.

paso3: Roles of SCRUM

  • Product Owner: The Product Owner is responsible for maximizing the value of the product. They define what needs to be built, manage and prioritize the Product Backlog, and ensure that the team is always working on the most important They also represent the stakeholders and make sure the product meets customer needs.
  • SCRUM Master:The Scrum Master supports the team by ensuring that Scrum is understood and properly followed. They act as a coach and facilitator, helping remove obstacles (impediments) that could slow the team down. They also promote good practices and help the team improve continuously.
  • Development team: The Development Team consists of the people who build the product. They are responsible for delivering a functional increment at the end of each Sprint. The team is self-organizing, meaning they decide how to do the work, and cross-functional, meaning they have all the skills needed to complete the tasks.

Paso5: SCRUM Events

Scrum events provide a structured and consistent framework that helps teams collaborate effectively, track progress, and continuously improve their work. Each event has a clear purpose and is time-boxed, meaning it has a maximum duration to ensure efficiency and focus. These events are not just meetings, but essential opportunities for inspection and adaptation, which are key principles of Scrum. All events take place within the Sprint, which acts as the container for all Scrum activities and ensures a continuous cycle of delivery and improvement.

Sprint:The Sprint is the central event where all the work happens. It is a fixed period, usually lasting between one and four weeks, during which the Scrum Team works toward a clear Sprint Goal and delivers a usable Increment. The Sprint provides stability and focus, allowing teams to make progress in a predictable way. While adjustments can be made during the Sprint, they must not compromise the goal, and quality must always be maintained.

Sprint Plainning: Sprint Planning marks the beginning of the Sprint and is used to define what work will be done and how it will be accomplished. During this event, the team collaborates to set a Sprint Goal, select items from the Product Backlog, and create a plan for delivering them. This ensures that everyone starts the Sprint aligned and with a clear direction.

Daily Scrum: The Daily Scrum is a short, daily meeting for the Developers to review progress toward the Sprint Goal and adjust their plan for the next 24 hours. It helps maintain alignment, improves communication, and allows the team to quickly identify and address any obstacles that could impact progress.

Sprint Review: The Sprint Review takes place at the end of the Sprint and focuses on evaluating the work completed. The team presents the Increment to stakeholders and gathers feedback, which is then used to update the Product Backlog and guide future work. This ensures that the product evolves based on real input and changing needs.

Sprint Retrospective: The Sprint Retrospective is the final event of the Sprint and is dedicated to improving how the team works. During this session, the team reflects on their processes, collaboration, and performance, identifying areas for improvement and defining actions to enhance future Sprints. This supports continuous learning and long-term effectiveness.

User stories

User Stories are a key element in Agile and Scrum because they describe features from the users perspective rather than focusing on technical details. Their main purpose is to clearly express what the user needs and why it is valuable, helping the team stay focused on delivering meaningful outcomes instead of just completing tasks. A User Story is usually written in a simple and structured format: “As a [user], I want [action], so that [benefit].” This format ensures that every feature has a clear purpose and is connected to a real user need. By working with User Stories, teams can better understand the product, prioritize effectively, and deliver value incrementally. User Stories are stored in the Product Backlog and evolve over time. At the beginning, they are often simple and high-level, but as the team learns more, they are refined and expanded through collaboration between the Product Owner and Developers. This continuous refinement helps reduce uncertainty and ensures that the team is always working on the most important features. Another important aspect of User Stories is that they are often supported by acceptance criteria. These criteria define the conditions that must be met for the story to be considered complete, helping avoid misunderstandings and ensuring quality. Additionally, User Stories encourage communication and teamwork, since they are meant to be discussed, not just implemented. This collaborative approach leads to better solutions and a more flexible development process. Overall, User Stories help teams focus on user value, improve communication, and adapt quickly to changes, making them an essential tool in modern product development.

Examples of User Stories

  1. As a user, I want to log in so that I can access my account
  2. As a customer, I want to track my order so that I know when it will arrive
  3. As a user, I want to reset my password so that I can recover my account
  4. As an admin, I want to manage users so that I can control access
  5. As a visitor, I want to search products so that I can find what I need quickly
  6. As a user, I want to save my preferences so that I have a personalized experience

Estimations of SCRUM

Estimations in Scrum are used to determine the amount of effort required to complete a task or a User Story. Instead of focusing on exact time, estimations help teams understand how complex, large, or uncertain a piece of work is. This allows the team to plan more effectively while accepting that in complex projects, it is difficult to predict everything with precision. In Scrum, estimations are typically done by the Developers, since they are responsible for completing the work. Rather than using hours, teams often use relative units such as story points. These points represent a combination of effort, complexity, and risk. For example, a simple task with little uncertainty will have a low estimation, while a complex task with many unknowns will have a higher one. Estimations are usually discussed during Sprint Planning or backlog refinement sessions. The team analyzes each User Story, shares perspectives, and reaches a common understanding of the work involved. Techniques like Planning Poker are often used to make the process collaborative and avoid bias. Over time, as the team gains experience, their estimations become more accurate, helping them better predict how much work they can complete in future Sprints. It is important to understand that estimations are not exact predictions or commitments. They are approximations that support planning and decision-making. Their main goal is to help the team work more efficiently, stay realistic about their capacity, and continuously improve their performance.

Paso 8: Recommended tools

In Scrum, teams use different tools to help them organize their work, collaborate efficiently, and track progress throughout the development process. These tools are not part of Scrum itself, but they support the framework by making it easier to manage tasks, visualize workflows, and communicate within the team. Choosing the right tool depends on the needs of the team and the complexity of the project. One of the most commonly used tools is Jira, which is designed specifically for Agile and Scrum teams. It allows teams to manage backlogs, plan sprints, assign tasks, and track progress in a structured way. Another popular tool is Trello, which uses a simple board and card system to organize tasks visually, making it easy to understand the workflow at a glance. Asana is also widely used, as it helps teams manage tasks, set deadlines, and collaborate effectively. Additionally, ClickUp is an all-in-one platform that combines task management, documentation, and communication features in a single tool. These tools help improve transparency, which is a key principle in Scrum, by making all work visible to the team. They also support better planning and coordination, allowing team members to stay aligned and respond quickly to changes. While Scrum can be implemented without any specific tool, using the right tools can significantly enhance productivity and team collaboration. Overall, recommended tools in Scrum are essential for supporting teamwork, improving organization, and ensuring that the development process runs smoothly and efficiently.
